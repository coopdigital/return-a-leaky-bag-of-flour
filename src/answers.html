Accessibility issues in 'Return a leaky bag of flour'

This is the answer sheet for the 'Return a leaky bag of flour' accessibility testing exercise.

Contact us page issues

1. We’ll be with you in two shakes of a lamb's tail to help is an idiom

Success Criterion 3.1.5 Reading Level (Level AAA)

Whether time pressured, stressed or we have low literacy levels. Plain and clear English helps us all. Idioms in particularly don’t translate well into other languages. See the readability guidelines on plain language for more.

2. Ask our social media team alt text too descriptive

Overly descriptive or repeated content is very frustrating for everyone but in this case especially for people using screen readers.

A guide to writing alt text

3. Contact us using our online form link text and alt text are the same

Screen reader users would hear the text twice. Only describe images that add to the story. Have a look at alt text that informs

4. Find store details and opening times has no alt text

Having no alt text means that the filename of the image get read out on a screenreader. And search engines have to guess what the image is.

5. Urgent and latest product recalls is an image

Success Criterion 1.4.5 Images of Text (Level AA)

Images of text don’t scale well and can become blurry when zoomed in. Text can often become to small to read when scaled down to a mobile screen. And page translator cannot translate image text.

Why Is It Important for Accessibility to Use Actual Text Instead of Images of Text?

6. Click here for more link isn’t descriptive

Success Criterion 1.3.1 Info and Relationships (Level A)

Context is lost when people tab through a page or our zoomed into the content. Having descriptive text also gives the user “Learn More” Links: You Can Do Better

Product returns page issues

1. Using the only a colour green and the red for what you can return

Success Criterion 1.3.3 Sensory Characteristics (Level A)

More than 99% of all colour blind people suffer from a red-green blindness and they generally have difficulty distinguishing between reds, greens, browns and oranges. Colours blindness affects roughly 1 in 12 men and 1 in 200 women. Read more about colour blindness in the artcile Lens Of Color

Return my flour form issues

1. When tabbing the address field is skipped

Success Criterion 2.4.3 Focus Order (Level A)

Whether you are a power user tabbing through a form. Or you can’t use a mouse because of a motor impairment or an injury. A logical order navigating a page helps everybody.

Keyboard-Only Navigation for Improved Accessibility

2. Focus states are clearly visible

Success Criterion 2.4.7 Focus Visible (Level AA)

Many people exclusively use keyboards to navigate, and rely on the focus state to know where they are on the page. If the focus state isn't visible (or hard to see), it's like trying to use a website with an invisible mouse cursor.

Accessible Focus Indicators: Something to :focus on

3. Postcode doesn’t have a label

Success Criterion 2.4.6 Headings and Labels (Level AA)

Without the label, a screenreader user would not be told they should enter a postcode. Form labels also provide visible descriptions and larger clickable targets for form controls.

The Anatomy of Accessible Forms: Best Practices

4. When clicked terms and conditions is opened it isn’t described

WAI ARIA - Disclosure Show / hide

When you click to view the terms you may get a visual cue as the terms and conditions display. If you don’t get that cue without a notification you can only assume something has happend.

5. Terms and conditions is in capital letters, big blocks of text with long line lengths d

Success Criterion 1.4.8 Visual Presentation (Level AAA)

If a casual reader gets tired of reading a long horizontal line, then they’re more likely to skim the left edge of the text. If an engaged reader gets tired of reading a long horizontal line, then they’re more likely to accidentally read the same line of text twice (a phenomenon known as “doubling”).

Size Matters: Balancing Line Length And Font Size In Responsive Web Design

6. Eligibility criteria radio buttons are too far away from the questions

Success Criterion 1.4.4 Resize text (Level AA)

There are around ten times as many people who use screen magnifiers than screen readers.

How to make your site accessible for screen magnifiers

Confirm your details page

1. Back button is a very light grey

Success Criterion 1.4.3 Contrast minimum (Level AA)

When the sun glares on your screen. Or the brightness is turned down on your screen. Good colour contrast benefits us all.

Colour contrast checker

Improving The Color Accessibility For Color-Blind Users

2. Hit area isn't too small for big fingers

Success Criterion 2.5.5 Target Size (Level AAA)

If you’ve ever typed a text message whilst on the go. Or you’ve used your phone with gloves on. Somebody will condered that the button needs to be big enough to be pressed.

Finger-Friendly Design: Ideal Mobile Touchscreen Target Sizes

Thank you page

1. Flour return confirmation is in a new style and aligned centrally

Success Criterion 1.4.8 Visual Level (AAA)

Left aligned text is easier to read than centered text for paragraphs. This is because when you center your text, the starting place of each line changes. This forces your users to work harder to find where each line begins to continue reading.

Align text to the left and keep a consistent layout

2. The animation of the chief is continious

Success Criterion 2.2.2 Pause, Stop, Hide Level (A)

Animated images can really enhance user experience and understanding, but there are limitations. If they're not designed in an accessible way, GIFs can be distracting or could even cause seizures.

How to make accessible animated images
